Borley Rectory

Borley Rectory, often referred to as the most haunted house in England, was a Gothic-style Victorian mansion located in the village of Borley, Essex. It gained fame for its alleged paranormal activity, which attracted significant attention from paranormal investigators and the media.

History

The rectory was built in 1862 by Reverend Henry Dawson Ellis Bull. It served as a residence for the rector of Borley Parish and his family. Paranormal activity was reported as early as the late 19th century. Residents and visitors claimed to have seen ghostly apparitions, heard unexplained noises, and experienced other strange phenomena.

Reverend Bull And The Haunting

  • The first reports of paranormal activity began shortly after the Bull family moved into the rectory. According to local lore, a ghostly nun was often seen wandering the grounds. She would glide silently through the garden, her face hidden beneath a dark veil. It was said that this nun was a member of a nearby convent who had been bricked up alive in the walls of the rectory after breaking her vows. The spectral figure would vanish into thin air when approached.
  • Reverend Bull himself reportedly saw the ghostly nun on several occasions and even built a summerhouse overlooking the area where she was most frequently seen. After his death, his successor, Reverend Smith, and his wife also experienced numerous paranormal events. Mrs. Smith once found a package of the reverend's clothes inexplicably strewn across the hallway, and on another occasion, she was nearly struck by a stone that flew out of nowhere. .
  • The rectory itself seemed to be alive with strange and inexplicable sounds. Late at night, the Bull family and their guests would hear the distinct sound of footsteps echoing through the empty hallways. Doors would creak open and slam shut by themselves, as if moved by unseen hands. These disturbances were particularly unsettling in the dead of night, when the house was otherwise silent. .
  • Another recurring phenomenon was the appearance of a phantom coach. Witnesses described hearing the distant rumble of carriage wheels and the clatter of horses' hooves on cobblestones, growing louder and louder until it seemed to be right outside the rectory. Those brave enough to look outside would see an old-fashioned coach, drawn by a team of spectral horses, careening through the grounds before vanishing into the darkness. As the years went by, the hauntings grew more intense. Objects would move of their own accord, sometimes flying across rooms with violent force. Stones and bricks would be thrown through the air, seemingly from nowhere. The Bull children often found their toys inexplicably moved or broken. One of the most unsettling manifestations was the appearance of strange, scrawled messages on the walls, written in a childlike hand. .

Ghost Writings and Harry Price

The ghost writings at Borley Rectory first gained attention during the tenancy of the Foyster family in the early 1930s. Marianne Foyster, the wife of the rector, reported finding numerous messages written on the walls. These writings often took the form of pleas for help, such as "Marianne, please help get" and "Rest." The messages appeared in various parts of the house, often in areas that were difficult to reach, adding to the mystery. News of the hauntings at Borley Rectory quickly spread, drawing the attention of the public and paranormal investigators alike. In 1929, the Daily Mirror newspaper sent a reporter to investigate the rectory, and they enlisted the help of Harry Price, a well-known paranormal researcher. Price's investigations brought even more attention to the haunted rectory. He conducted séances, experiments, and detailed observations, all of which seemed to confirm the presence of supernatural entities.

During his time at Borley Rectory, Price claimed to witness and record numerous unexplained phenomena, including ghostly apparitions, mysterious noises, and objects moving on their own. He organized séances and experiments, capturing what he believed were genuine supernatural occurrences. His investigations at Borley Rectory were widely publicized, significantly contributing to its reputation as the most haunted house in England. Price documented his findings in books and articles, with his work on Borley Rectory becoming some of the most famous and controversial in the field of paranormal research. Despite skepticism and criticism from some quarters, Harry Price's association with Borley Rectory remains a cornerstone of his legacy in the study of the supernatural. Price's investigations into Borley Rectory's ghost writings are among the most intriguing aspects of his work. These mysterious writings appeared on the walls of the rectory and were a key piece of evidence for Price's claims of paranormal activity. .

When Price began his investigations in earnest in 1930, he documented the ghost writings extensively. Price was fascinated by these messages and spent considerable time and effort trying to understand their origin. He meticulously recorded their content, location, and any circumstances surrounding their appearance. Price believed that these writings were a genuine form of spirit communication, potentially from a nun who was said to have been bricked up in the walls of the rectory as punishment for breaking her vows. Price conducted several experiments to test the authenticity of the writings. One notable method involved sealing rooms where the writings had appeared, only to find new messages in those same areas later. He also brought in handwriting experts and attempted to match the ghostly script with known samples from the Foyster family and other residents, but no definitive conclusions were reached. The ghost writings attracted significant public interest and were widely reported in the media, contributing to Borley Rectory's reputation. However, they also attracted skepticism. Critics argued that the writings could have been a hoax, perhaps perpetrated by Marianne Foyster or another resident seeking attention. Despite these claims, no conclusive evidence was ever found to prove the writings were fraudulent. Mr Price died in 1948, and since then, many sceptics have dissected his work, explaining all sound phenomena and other phenomena as misinterpreted natural occurrences. Believe what you will, but all reports stretching over 100 years seem to cement its place as one of the most haunted houses in Britain and maybe even the world.

List Of Owners of Borley Rectory

  • Reverend Henry Dawson Ellis Bull (1863-1892) Occupation Period: 1863 to 1892 Details: Reverend Bull had the rectory built in 1862 and moved in with his family in 1863. The Bull family reported paranormal activity early on, including sightings of a nun's ghost.
  • Reverend Harry Foyster Bull (1892-1927) Occupation Period: 1892 to 1927 Details: Son of Henry Dawson Ellis Bull, Harry Foyster Bull continued to live in the rectory after his father's death. The paranormal activity reportedly continued during his tenure.
  • Reverend Guy Eric Smith (1928-1929) Occupation Period: October 1928 to July 1929 Details: Reverend Smith and his wife moved into the rectory in 1928. They experienced various strange occurrences and found a skull in a cupboard, which prompted them to contact the Daily Mirror, leading to Harry Price's initial investigation.
  • Reverend Lionel Foyster (1930-1935) Occupation Period: October 1930 to 1935 Details: Reverend Lionel Foyster, a cousin of the Bull family, moved into the rectory with his wife Marianne and adopted daughter Adelaide. They experienced significant paranormal activity, including the famous ghost writings. Lionel documented many of these events in his diary.
  • Harry Price (1937-1938) Occupation Period: May 1937 to May 1938 Details: Harry Price rented the rectory for one year to conduct a series of investigations. He brought in 48 official observers and conducted various experiments to document and understand the reported phenomena.
  • Captain W. H. Gregson (1939) Occupation Period: 1938 to 1939 Details: Captain Gregson bought the rectory, intending to convert it into a tourist attraction. However, the rectory was severely damaged by fire in February 1939, shortly after he moved in. The cause of the fire was never conclusively determined. The rectory was left in ruins after the fire in 1939 and was eventually demolished in 1944.
